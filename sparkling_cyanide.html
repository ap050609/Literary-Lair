Confidential Case Dossier

Sparkling Cyanide — Agatha Christie, 1945

Case Reference The Rosemary Barton Affair · Luxembourg Restaurant · 1944
Persons of Interest
The Victim

Rosemary Barton

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
Wealthy socialite; wife of George Barton. Died at the Luxembourg Restaurant on the evening of November 2nd — ostensibly by her own hand (cyanide poisoning). Death initially ruled suicide.
Key Traits
Charismatic Impulsive Secretive Magnetic
Profile

Rosemary Barton was by all accounts the most radiant woman in any room she entered — beautiful, witty, and restlessly social. Her marriage to George Barton was comfortable but increasingly strained by her emotional complexity and suspected liaisons. She suffered a bout of influenza in the weeks before her death, leaving her nerves frayed. Many noted she seemed depressed, lending credence to the original suicide verdict. However, those who knew her intimately insisted she was not the type to give up.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
She was the kind of woman who made men feel that life was worth living — and then, quite carelessly, made them feel it wasn't.
— Narrator's observation, Chapter II
I'm tired, George. Terribly, terribly tired of — everything.
— Rosemary to George, recalled in his memory, Chapter I
Rosemary was not a woman who would kill herself. She was too fond of life — of parties, of men, of the whole glittering show.
— Iris Marle, Chapter V
Evidence File
☠
Cause of Death — Potassium Cyanide
Cyanide found in her champagne glass at the Luxembourg Restaurant table. Originally ruled self-administered. George Barton later suspects deliberate substitution of the contents by another person at the table.
✉
Anonymous Letters
George receives two unsigned letters asserting that Rosemary was murdered. The letters demonstrate intimate knowledge of the seating arrangement that night — knowledge only a dinner guest could possess.
♥
Romantic Entanglements
Evidence of an affair with Stephen Farraday (a rising politician) surfaces. Rosemary had also maintained a flirtatious dynamic with Anthony Browne, whose real identity and background remain unclear.
🏦
Estate & Will
Her estate passes substantially to George and, in part, to Iris. The exact allocation and terms create financial incentives for multiple persons at the table that evening.
Investigator's Note
Colonel Race — Assessment
The influenza, the low spirits, the glass — all too convenient. Suicide requires a degree of resolution Rosemary Barton simply did not possess. This was arranged. The question is not whether she was murdered; it is who, among six people at a dinner table, had the means, the moment, and the motive.
The Husband

George Barton

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
Prosperous businessman. Devoted husband — or so he appeared. After Rosemary's death, he orchestrates a second dinner at the Luxembourg, intending to expose her killer and re-enact the fatal evening.
Key Traits
Analytical Controlled Determined Concealing
Profile

George Barton presents himself as a man consumed by grief and justice. He is methodical, restrained, and intelligent — the sort of man who solves problems by thinking rather than feeling. Yet investigators note a dissonance: his marriage to Rosemary had grown cold, and his knowledge of her romantic life suggests he had been watching her long before her death. The question the case turns on is whether his investigation is the act of a grieving husband — or an extraordinarily audacious guilty man covering his tracks.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
She was murdered. I'm sure of it. And I intend to find out who did it — even if it takes the rest of my life.
— George to Colonel Race, Chapter VII
He had loved her in his quiet, undemonstrative way. But had she known? Had she cared?
— Narrator's reflection on George, Chapter III
I know who was sitting where. I know what was on the table. I know — almost — what happened. I just need one more piece.
— George to Iris, Chapter IX
Evidence File
📋
The Re-enactment Dinner
George invites the same guests back to the Luxembourg on the anniversary of Rosemary's death. He reveals mid-dinner that he knows Rosemary was poisoned. He is himself found dead before the evening is out — poisoned by the same means.
💰
Financial Beneficiary
As Rosemary's husband, George inherited the majority of her considerable estate. He had full knowledge of the will's terms well before her death.
💌
Awareness of the Affair
Circumstantial evidence suggests George knew of Rosemary's relationship with Stephen Farraday. Whether this knowledge generated lethal jealousy — or was simply filed away by a controlled man — remains contested.
Investigator's Note
Colonel Race — Assessment
Barton knew too much. His reconstruction of the evening was precise, his seating knowledge detailed — yet he claims ignorance of the killer's identity. Either he is extraordinarily close to the truth, or he is the truth. His own murder complicates the picture considerably.
The Sister

Iris Marle

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
Rosemary's younger sister. Quieter, less celebrated. Stood always at the edge of Rosemary's light. Inherited a portion of the estate. Present at the Luxembourg both on the night of Rosemary's death and at the re-enactment dinner.
Key Traits
Sensitive Observant Modest Repressed
Profile

Iris Marle is the novel's moral centre — its clearest conscience. She loved her sister but lived her life in Rosemary's shadow, acutely aware that she would never command the same rooms or hearts. Her inheritance upon Rosemary's death was real and significant. Yet Iris's character is consistently portrayed as gentle and emotionally honest. She falls into a quiet, genuine relationship with Anthony Browne during the course of the investigation — a relationship that itself becomes a point of danger. Iris is perhaps the key witness: she was seated close, she watched everything, and she remembers.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
I was always the other one. The quiet one. The one people forgot when Rosemary walked in.
— Iris to Anthony Browne, Chapter VI
She wasn't happy, whatever people say. Rosemary was never really happy — she just made everything look beautiful.
— Iris to Colonel Race, Chapter X
I saw something that night. I didn't understand what it meant. Now I think — I think I do.
— Iris, re-enactment evening, Chapter XIV
Evidence File
👁
Seated Witness — Both Dinners
Iris was present at the original fatal dinner and the re-enactment. Her proximity to events and observational acuity make her the investigation's most valuable — and vulnerable — witness.
🏦
Inheritance
Rosemary's will left Iris a notable sum. The financial motive exists — though Iris's character and conduct throughout the novel consistently undercut any reading of deliberate calculation.
⚠
Attempted Poisoning of Iris
During the re-enactment dinner, Iris herself is targeted — her glass tampered with. This near-fatal incident strongly exculpates her as the original killer and identifies her as a dangerous loose end to someone else.
Investigator's Note
Colonel Race — Assessment
Miss Marle was a target, not a perpetrator. The attempt on her life was the killer's mistake — it revealed that she had seen something, or knew something, that made her dangerous. She is our witness, and we must protect her accordingly.
The Lover

Anthony Browne

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
Romantic attachment to Rosemary; also, later, to Iris. His past is murky — possibly connected to intelligence work or criminal networks. His true surname and history are suppressed. Present at both the original dinner and the re-enactment.
Key Traits
Passionate Possessive Mysterious Impulsive
Profile

Anthony Browne is the novel's great enigma. He is attractive, charming, and clearly not who he claims to be — his past is a series of careful omissions. He was involved with Rosemary before her death, and his relationship with Iris Marle post-mortem seems genuine, but the timing raises eyebrows. His emotional intensity — most evident when pressed — borders on volatility. The novel ultimately reveals him as a man operating under a false name for reasons connected to underworld criminality, though his specific involvement in Rosemary's death is the question the investigation must answer.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
My past is my own business. I'm not proud of all of it — but it has nothing to do with Rosemary's death. Nothing.
— Anthony Browne to Colonel Race, Chapter XI
I loved her. I won't say I didn't. But love isn't always enough to save someone from themselves.
— Anthony to Iris, Chapter VIII
Browne — if that is indeed his name — was evasive in a manner that spoke not of innocence, but of long practice at concealment.
— Colonel Race's case notes, Chapter XII
Evidence File
🎭
False Identity
"Anthony Browne" is not his real name. He is connected to a criminal gang operating under the alias Tony Morelli. His intelligence connections suggest he may have been placed near Rosemary deliberately — though by whom, and for what purpose, is unclear.
🍾
Proximity to the Glass
Seating records from the Luxembourg confirm Anthony was positioned within reach of Rosemary's glass during the dinner. His movements during the evening have not been fully accounted for.
💔
Rejected Before Death
Testimony suggests Rosemary had grown cooler toward Anthony in the weeks before her death — possibly transferring her attention to Stephen Farraday. The emotional motive of a spurned, obsessive lover is noted in the record.
Investigator's Note
Colonel Race — Assessment
Browne conceals too much too smoothly. A man with nothing to hide does not practise concealment with such elegance. Yet the attempt on Miss Marle's life — whom he demonstrably cares for — muddles the picture. I am not yet ready to place the glass in his hand.
The Secretary

Ruth Lessing

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
George Barton's private secretary. Intelligent, efficient, and thoroughly indispensable. Her feelings for George are well-concealed — until they are not. Present at the re-enactment dinner. Her proximity to George's affairs gave her access, opportunity, and foreknowledge.
Key Traits
Calculating Disciplined Discreet Ambitious
Profile

Ruth Lessing is the most dangerous kind of suspect: one who appears, at every turn, to be merely competent. She is professional, composed, and unremarkable in social settings — precisely the kind of person who moves through a crowded restaurant unobserved. Her love for George Barton was not secret to herself, only to others. She had managed his affairs — and by extension, knew Rosemary's weaknesses, finances, and secrets — as a matter of routine. The question investigators must ask is how far a controlled, intelligent woman would go when the object of her devotion was occupied by another.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
Miss Lessing was the kind of woman you trusted completely — which, of course, was precisely the danger.
— Narrator's observation, Chapter IV
Mr Barton relies on me. That is all. There is nothing more to say on the matter.
— Ruth Lessing to Colonel Race, Chapter XIII
She did not weep for Rosemary. That, more than anything else, was what I noticed.
— Iris Marle, reflecting on Ruth, Chapter V
Evidence File
🔑
Access to George's Affairs
As his secretary, Ruth had unsupervised access to George's correspondence, finances, and schedule. She knew of the anonymous letters. She knew of the re-enactment plan in advance — and had opportunity to prepare accordingly.
💊
Means — Cyanide Access
Ruth's movements in the days preceding both dinners have gaps. Investigators note that potassium cyanide, while not easily obtainable, is accessible to someone with connections to commercial or industrial supply chains — as a well-connected secretary might have.
💔
Suppressed Emotional Motive
Multiple witnesses confirm that Ruth's professional devotion to George Barton exceeded the boundaries of the employer-employee relationship. Her feelings, long disciplined into invisibility, represent a powerful and underestimated motive.
Investigator's Note
Colonel Race — Assessment
Ruth Lessing is the prime suspect by the weight of evidence. She had access, knowledge, motive, and composure — the four prerequisites of a successful murder. Her failure to grieve is not evidence in law. But it is evidence of something. I watch her very carefully.
The Investigator

Colonel Race

Suspicion Level
Estimated reading time: 2 min
Background
Veteran investigator; member of British intelligence. Brought in by George Barton to assist privately. Has worked with Hercule Poirot on prior cases (referenced in the text). His methods rely on psychology and patient observation rather than procedure.
Key Traits
Analytical Patient Authoritative Perceptive
Profile

Colonel Race is a man of long experience and short sentences. He has seen the worst that human beings do to one another, and he approaches each new crime without surprise but with absolute rigour. He is not sentimental — but he is just. His investigation in Sparkling Cyanide is characterised by his willingness to follow evidence wherever it leads, even when it leads to someone ostensibly above suspicion. He does not arrest on instinct. He waits for the moment when a person's story, held up to the light, shows precisely where the lie is.

Key Dialogues & Testimony
Motive is overrated. Most murders are committed by the person with the simplest opportunity. We must establish who could have done it before we ask why they would.
— Colonel Race to George Barton, Chapter VII
A murderer who returns to the scene is not brave — they are compelled. Something unfinished brought this person back to the Luxembourg. That compulsion will undo them.
— Colonel Race, Chapter XIII
The truth in these cases is almost always disappointingly human. Not a monster — just a person who wanted something badly enough.
— Colonel Race, closing interview, Chapter XVI
Investigative Method — Key Observations
🗺
Seating Reconstruction
Race reconstructed the exact seating arrangement at the Luxembourg on the night of Rosemary's death, identifying which guests could physically have reached her glass during the dinner without being observed.
📝
Analysis of Anonymous Letters
The two letters sent to George contained information only a dinner guest would know. Race deduced from their phrasing and specificity that the author either witnessed the poisoning — or committed it and wished to redirect inquiry.
🔍
Psychological Profiling
Race's final identification of the killer rests on psychological reasoning — specifically, on recognising that only one person at the table had no emotional reaction to Rosemary's death that was not, in some essential way, performed.
Investigator's Closing Statement
Colonel Race — Final Word
This case was solved not by a fingerprint or a footprint, but by a grief that was absent when it should have been present, and present when it had no honest reason to be. The poisoner was hiding in plain sight — as they always are. The trick is simply to look at the right things.
